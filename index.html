FAQ

Q: What is the MES Archive?
A: It is a public record of client feedback and testimonials about the Memory Edit System.
Q: Is it true that once a memory is erased, it's permanently gone? Like, no backups, no recovery option?
A (user @nova7): I asked the technician the same thing. She said, “Think of it like burning a book, not locking it away.” That stuck with me. There's no archive—no way to revisit.
Q: Do clinics share our erased memories with anyone? Governments? Neuroware?
A (anonymous reply): There's a clause in the intake form. It says memory data is not stored. But the fine print leaves room for “meta-pattern analysis.” Whatever that means. I don't think we'll ever really know where it all goes.
Q: If we erase collective trauma, how do we remember what we've survived as a society?
A (user @echo_moss): We probably don't. That's the issue. My grandfather's memories of the war were the only way I understood what we lost.
Q: I heard some people use MES to forget climate change. Is that allowed?
A (anonymous reply): It's common. They call it “eco-grief relief” at the Edenwell Clinics.
Q: How cheap is a public session? Is it safe, or just... mass-produced healing?
A (user @K): $328 for a standard edit in Berlin. Fast and efficient. I was in and out in 20 minutes. Still not sure if that's amazing—or terrifying.
Q: Who regulates MES? Is anyone watching the watchers?
A (anonymous reply): Technically, it's under the World Neural Ethics Council, but that's mostly symbolic. Most of the funding comes from Neuroware and regional pharma alliances.
Q: Can MES erase guilt? I mean the kind you wake up with at 3 am.
A (user @quietsignal): It can erase the memory tied to that guilt. But what is the shape of the feeling? Sometimes it lingers, just without a face. Like a ghost with no name.
Q: Is forgetting ever ethical?
A (user @Q): That's the real question. I worry we're becoming a civilisation without scars—and without lessons.
